# Structured Insights — Owner Persona (Mobile Track & Trace Study)

## Persona
Owner (Business Owner / Fleet Owner)

## Market
Transportation companies (various sizes: 1-51+ trucks), primarily transport and freight forwarding companies across Czech Republic, Slovakia, Hungary, Portugal, Romania, Spain, Poland

## Research Type
Quantitative survey study

## Sample Size
168 owners and decision-makers (119 owners, 15 dispatchers, 13 fleet managers, 12 administrative, others)

---

## Core Jobs

- Manage fleet operations and vehicle tracking
- Communicate with drivers effectively
- Monitor vehicle locations and goods
- Plan and optimize fuel costs
- Provide technology tools (phones, data packages) to drivers
- Ensure operational visibility without invading driver privacy
- Make decisions about telematics and fleet management solutions
- Coordinate refueling locations and schedules
- Track vehicles (not necessarily drivers) for operational control
- Manage costs and optimize fleet utilization

---

## Pain Points

- **Driver privacy concerns**: Significant concerns that drivers may resist tracking, especially outside working hours
- **Misunderstanding of tracking concepts**: TrackAndTrace concept (driver sharing location) not well understood, leading to privacy concerns
- **Mixed technology policies**: Inconsistent approaches to phone provision and data packages across companies
- **Lack of advanced solutions**: Need for more advanced fleet monitoring and management solutions beyond basic tools
- **Driver distraction concerns**: Worry that fuel price display or other features may distract drivers
- **Pre-planning vs. real-time data**: Fuel refueling often planned in advance, making real-time fuel prices less useful
- **Vehicle tracking confidence**: Need for solutions that provide confidence in vehicle tracking
- **Scalability needs**: Need for solutions that accommodate different fleet sizes and company maturity levels
- **Integration challenges**: Managing multiple tools (Transics, Dynafleet, Arobs, Volvo Connect, Frotcom) separately
- **Cost management**: Need to optimize fuel costs and fleet expenses

---

## Frictions

- Balancing operational visibility with driver privacy
- Managing drivers who use private phones for work (51% of companies)
- Inconsistent policies on internet/roaming packages (32% don't offer any)
- Switching between multiple communication channels (phone calls, WhatsApp, SMS, email)
- Distinction between vehicle tracking (acceptable) vs. driver tracking (privacy concerns)
- Pre-planned refueling making real-time fuel price features less useful
- Driver resistance to tracking on personal devices
- Concerns about driver internet data usage for tracking
- Lack of integrated solutions combining GPS, payment, tachograph management
- Managing fines, legal support, and compliance issues

---

## Monetization Signals

- Willingness to invest in scalable, modular telematics solutions
- Interest in varied feature packages for different fleet sizes
- Value placed on vehicle tracking and operational visibility
- Recognition that advanced fleet management tools are needed
- Interest in integrated solutions (GPS, payment, tachograph, fine management)
- Willingness to provide data packages and company phones to drivers
- Preference for solutions that target owners/dispatchers rather than drivers
- Interest in fuel cost optimization features

---

## Behavioral Signals

- Prefer vehicle tracking over driver tracking
- Use phone calls as primary communication method (77%)
- Growing adoption of instant messaging (WhatsApp, Telegram) - 55%
- Provide company phones to drivers (49%) or allow private phone use (51%)
- Already using similar tracking solutions (27% of companies)
- Plan refueling locations in advance rather than using real-time prices
- Use specialized fleet management tools (Transics, Dynafleet, Arobs, Volvo Connect, Frotcom)
- Prefer work-hours-only tracking to respect driver privacy
- Value modular, scalable solutions over one-size-fits-all
- Geographic differences in communication preferences and technology adoption

---

## Emotional Signals

- Concern about driver privacy and resistance to tracking
- Frustration with misunderstanding of tracking concepts
- Appreciation for vehicle-centric solutions
- Relief when solutions respect privacy boundaries
- Interest in features that optimize costs and operations
- Skepticism about driver-focused tracking features
- Preference for solutions that provide confidence and reliability
- Concern about driver distraction from additional features
- Appreciation for integrated, comprehensive solutions
- Trust in vehicle-based tracking systems (GPS, tachograph)

- "I don't like it - it's not useful for them as the owner or dispatcher plans refueling places in advance" (23 owners)
- "I don't like it - they may be distracted" (6 owners)
- "I like it!" (fuel prices)
- "The driver has nothing to do with fuel price"
- "The dispatcher tells him where to refuel, the driver has nothing to do with the fuel price, he doesn't pay."
- "It doesn't bother me but for bigger companies it could be a problem. I think it is enough if it only shows the cheapest stations."

- "I like it, but it would be great to track them only at work/during work-related activities" (28 companies)
- "I like it, but I'm afraid that drivers don't want to be tracked at all" (26 companies)
- "I don't like the idea" (location tracking - 24 companies)

- "We have satellite vehicle tracking."

- How do owners prioritize between vehicle tracking and driver tracking features?
- What is the minimum feature set required for small vs. large companies?
- How can fuel price features be designed to avoid driver distraction?
- What is the ROI threshold for telematics and fleet management investments?
- How do geographic differences in communication preferences impact solution design?
- What is the optimal approach to phone and data package provision for drivers?
